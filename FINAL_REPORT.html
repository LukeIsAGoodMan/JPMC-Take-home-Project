The core insight The final deliverable is not "just a model." It is a two-layer targeting design: the classifier tells you who to prioritize; the segmentation tells you how to approach them. Neither alone is the full answer.

Classifier: CatBoost on a reduced feature set (31 features). Tuned with a 27-configuration grid search. Three operating modes (precision-leaning, balanced, recall-leaning) give the business control over the targeting tradeoff.

Segmentation: K-Prototypes clustering on 17 mixed-type features produces 3 interpretable life-stage groups — seniors, youth/dependents, and working-age householders — each suggesting distinct marketing approaches.

1. Problem Framing

A retail client wants to identify high-income individuals for premium product targeting. Census data provides demographic and employment variables but no direct transaction data. Income above $50,000 serves as a proxy target — not the ultimate business objective.

Income is a proxy What the client actually cares about is purchasing power, product affinity, and propensity to respond. The >$50K label is a useful first approximation, but a production system should validate against real business outcomes.

5. Segmentation Modeling

Why Segment Beyond the Classifier?

The classifier tells you who is likely high-income. It does not tell you who these people are or how to approach them. Segmentation provides the action-design layer that converts a statistical score into a marketing plan.

Fit on a random 30,000-row subsample (seed=42), assigned to all 199,523 rows. In evaluation outputs, k=3 was stable while k≥4 produced micro-clusters. To make outlier handling explicit, the clustering step requested k=4, which isolated a tiny micro-cluster (<0.2%). A deterministic absorption rule then merged it into its nearest neighbor, producing the final 3-segment solution.

Governance Target (_target) was excluded from clustering input — used only after clustering for profiling. Weight (_weight) was excluded from clustering input — used only for population-level segment sizing.

Final Segments

Segment 0: Senior/Retired Householders

20% of population · 2.2% >$50K · 0.35× enrichment
  • Mean age 63, mostly married, not in workforce
  • High school education dominant
  • ~2 weeks/year worked

Marketing: Retirement products, estate planning, Medicare supplements. Direct mail and phone channels.

Segment 1: Youth & Dependents

33% of population · 0.05% >$50K · 0.01× enrichment
  • Mean age 11, children and young dependents
  • Not in labor force

47% of population · 12.5% >$50K · 2.1× enrichment

6. Integrated Targeting Strategy

The Two-Layer Model

LayerWhat It DoesQuestion It Answers
ClassifierScores each person by income likelihood (0–1)Who should we prioritize?
Threshold PolicyConverts score into a targeting decisionWhere do we draw the line?
SegmentationGroups people by life-stage and household typeHow should we approach them?

Example Operational Workflows

Scenario 1: Premium credit card launch (budget-constrained) Use precision-leaning threshold (0.45). Contact only the highest-confidence prospects. Within that group, customize messaging by segment: investment language for Segment 2, retirement messaging for Segment 0.
Scenario 2: Financial literacy campaign (broad reach) Use recall-leaning threshold (0.20). Cast a wide net to find as many high-income individuals as possible. Segment determines channel: digital for Segment 2, community events for Segment 0, family-targeted for Segment 1.
Scenario 3: Market sizing (no outreach needed) Use segmentation alone. Report weighted population shares to size market opportunity by life-stage group. The classifier's probability score provides a continuous dimension for sub-targeting within the large working-age segment.

7. Key Insights

1. Threshold is a business policy, not a model parameter The model's ranking quality (PR-AUC 0.70) is constant. What changes with threshold is the precision–recall tradeoff. The business should choose which error is more costly: missing a high-income person, or wasting outreach on someone who isn't.
2. Marginal cases are where the value concentrates People far from the decision boundary are easy calls. The hard — and most business-relevant — decisions are about individuals the model scores near the threshold. A hard 0/1 label hides this nuance; a score with policy preserves it.
3. Income is a proxy target The >$50K label approximates purchasing power but is not what a retail client ultimately optimizes for. In production, validate against response rates, conversion, or lifetime value — not just income classification accuracy.
4. Segmentation converts score into action A probability score alone doesn't tell a campaign manager what creative to use or what product to offer. Segments provide the "who are these people" context that makes the score actionable.
5. Governance and interpretability beat complexity The reduced feature set outperformed the full set. Calibration wasn't needed. The 3-segment solution is more useful than a 7-cluster one with micro-groups. At every stage, simpler and more auditable won.
